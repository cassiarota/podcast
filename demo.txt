CHAPTER ONE

The lighthouse keeper had counted the waves for thirty years. Each morning he
walked the spiral stair, sixty-eight steps from the cottage to the lamp, and
each night he descended again with a lantern in one hand and the day's notes
in the other. The cliffs below his window had once been twice as tall, the
sea had once been twice as quiet, and he himself had once been twice as
certain of what mattered.

CHAPTER TWO

On the morning the message arrived, the gulls were unusually still. The boy
who brought it had run all the way from the village, his cheeks the color of
the rosehips that lined the path. He held out the folded paper without a
word, and the keeper, sensing what the silence held, did not unfold it for
some time.

When at last he read the message, he folded it again carefully, put it in
his coat pocket, climbed the sixty-eight steps, and lit the lamp two hours
early.

CHAPTER THREE

The lamp burned through the afternoon and into the evening and out the
other side of the night. The keeper sat beside it the whole time, watching
the horizon as though it might forget its job and slip away. He did not eat.
He did not sleep. He counted, instead, the long slow waves rolling in from
the open sea, and when he reached ten thousand he stopped counting and
began again.
